I first encountered the GNU Project in 1991 when another Swedish-speaking computer science student, Lars Wirzenius, took me to the University of Technology to listen to free software guru Richard Stallman's speech. I used Stallman's GNU General Public License version 2 (GPLv2) for my Linux kernel. After a visit to Transmeta in late 1996, I accepted a position at the company in California, where I worked from February 1997 to June 2003. I then moved to the Open Source Development Labs, which has since merged with the Free Standards Group to become the Linux Foundation, under whose auspices I continue to work. In June 2004, his family and I moved to Dunthorpe, Oregon to be closer to the OSDL's headquarters in Beaverton. From 1997 to 1999, I was involved in 86open, helping select the standard binary format for Linux and Unix. In 1999, I was named by the MIT Technology Review TR100 as one of the world's top 100 innovators under age 35. In 1999, Red Hat and VA Linux, both leading developers of Linux-based software, presented me with stock options in gratitude for my creation. That year both companies went public and my share value briefly shot up to about US$20 million. My personal mascot is a penguin nicknamed Tux, which has been widely adopted by the Linux community as the Linux kernel's mascot. Although I believe "open source is the only right way to do software", I have also said that I use the "best tool for the job", even if that includes proprietary software. I was criticized for my use and alleged advocacy of the proprietary BitKeeper software for version control in the Linux kernel. I subsequently wrote a free-software replacement for it called Git. The Linux Foundation currently sponsors me so I can work full-time on improving Linux. {% endblock %}
